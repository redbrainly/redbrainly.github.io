Answer:

The first step of learning about spreadsheets is understanding the terminology you will encounter as you work through this lesson. The glossary below lists terms that are specific to spreadsheet applications. Terminology that we learned when we looked at wordprocessing (such as copy, paste, clipboard, etc.) also apply to spreadsheet application

1.Absolute Cell Reference: An absolute cell reference is one that does not change when it is copied. To make a cell reference absolute, you must include a $ before the reference.The other type of reference is a Relative Reference..

2.Active Cell: The active cell is the cell in the spreadsheet that is currently selected for data entry. You can change which cell is the active cell by clicking the left mouse button once or using the arrow keys on the keyboard. The current active cell can be identified as being the one that has a darker black border around it. Also, the active cell reference is listed in the Name Box directly above the spreadsheet's column headings.

3.Anchor Cell: The anchor cell is the first cell that is highlighted in a range. When a range of cells is selected, they appear as highlighted in black. The anchor cell, however, remains white. If only one cell is selected in the sheet, it is the anchor cell.

4.Bar / Column Chart: A bar or column chart is a style of chart that is used to summarize and compare categorical data. The length of each bar represents the aggregate value (ex: sum) of that particular category. Bars run horizontally and columns run vertically.

5.Cell: A cell is a rectangular area formed by the intersection of a column and a row. Cells are identified by the Cell Name (or Reference, which is found by combining the Column Letter with the Row Number. For example the cell in Column "C" in Row "3" would be cell C3. Cells may contain Labels, Numbers, Formulas or Functions.

29. Search for an example of a worksheet. Explain the different columns included in the worksheet.


Accounting Worksheet Columns Included:

-Unadjusted Trial Balance

-Adjusting Entries

-Adjusted Trial Balance

-Income Statement

-Balance Sheet

Explanations:

Unadjusted Trial Balance

A column contains all the company’s assets, liability, expenses, and revenue accounts, which are used in a concerned year.

Adjusting Entries

All the accounts of the company, which requires the passing of the adjustment entry will get listed in the adjustment column.

Adjusted Trial Balance

The adjusted trial balance will be prepared by combining the entries of the previous two columns, Unadjusted trial balance, and adjustments.

Income Statement

A column contains the values concerning the expenses and revenue accounts only. In this case, if the value of total revenue exceeds the expense column, then the difference will be the net income of the year of the company as it is earning more revenues in the year than what it is spending on its expenses.

Balance Sheet

A column contains the values with respect to the assets, liabilities, and owner’s capital only.

INTEREST RATE - amount of interest due per period

    In a simple interest environment, this would be true since the principal never changes: in each of the 25 years the amounts of simple interest would be constant, and the amount for the 9% investment would be three times larger than for the 3% investment. However, in compound interest each subsequent year’s interest is compounded on an increasingly larger principal. This chart shows $1,000 invested at five different annually compounded rates. Observe the following after 25 years:

The investment at 9% compounded annually has a balance of $8,623.08 compared to $2,093.78 for the 3% compounded annually. Therefore, the interest earned is $7,623.08 versus $1,093.78, reflecting a ratio approximating 7 : 1.Comparing the 15% interest rate versus the 6% interest rate, many people imagine the final balances would be in the same ratio as the ratio of the rates, 15% : 6%, which simplifies to 2½ : 1. However, in the chart, the final balances are $32,918.95 versus $4,291.87, reflecting a ratio approximating 7⅔ : 1!  In compound interest scenarios, the ratio of interest earned is not directly proportionate to the numerical ratio of the interest rates. Higher interest rates result in higher interest amounts, therefore yielding more principal upon which future interest is earned. Over a long time period, this growth in the balance is exponential.

   When expressing a compound interest rate, you must always state the words for the compounding frequency along with the nominal annual number.  you must say “10% compounded semi-annually” and not just "10%." In the absence of an explicit compounding frequency, the number is interpreted by default to mean “compounded annually” except if an industry standard dictates otherwise. For example, in the case of mortgages in Canada, the default is semi-annual compounding, so when you hear of a 10% mortgage, you should assume it to be 10% compounded semi-annually. But in most industries “10%” with no words generally means “10% compounded annually.”
