14. characteristics and function of gothic painting.


Gothic art was a type of medieval art that evolved out of Romanesque art in the 12th century AD in northern France, followed by the parallel growth of Gothic architecture. It spread across Western Europe, and most of Southern and Central Europe, in Italy, never quite effacing more traditional types. The sophisticated International Gothic courtyard style developed in the late 14th century, which continued to evolve until the late 15th century.

In the 12th century the gothic style of art began. Christian symbolism was typical of early Gothic art. The Virgin Mary and Christ changed from the iconic Byzantine form to a figure that was more forgiving and approachable. The paintings were darkly mysterious and charged with an etheric force of feeling. Gothic painters were storytellers above all, producing their craft for recounting both Biblical and secular tales and legends.  

Gothic painters, as for sculpture, used techniques such as tempera and oil, painted on boards and made numerous mural paintings. Drawing was very important for them as it was responsible for delimiting the different forms, marking models and generating compositional rhythms. Light has been an important element in emphasizing volume. Colors were key elements, and use of different ternary ranges to fill all the symbols with great color.

Gothic painting is primarily characterized by a formalized sweetness and grace, elegance, and richness of detail, and an idealized quality.
