Poverty in the Philippines is more pervasive and chronic than in surrounding ASEAN (Association of South East Asian Nations) t nations. While the Philippines' poverty rate has fallen over the previous quarter century, the decline has been slower than in other ASEAN nations. Past economic policies that slowed growth by discriminating against agriculture and discouraging investment in human capital are partially to blame for the Philippines' sluggish progress in lowering the incidence of poverty.  These policies reinforced powerful interest groups that prevented or delayed economic progress.

In contrast, the Philippines initiated political and economic reforms in the late 1980s and early 1990s, and since 1994, annual GDP growth has increased to approximately 5 %.  With quicker growth, the proportion of Filipinos living below the poverty line is dropping, but agricultural reform and higher investment in human capital would allow for a more significant decline.

22. how quantitative research can contribute to poverty reduction?


The Contribution of Quantitative Research on Poverty Reduction

Two forms of testing are frequently the focus of quantitative research: (1) Correlative, and (2) Comparative Causation. Both can be used to discuss reducing poverty. Testing for correlations focuses on the relationship between the outcome and potential interval-type predictors of poverty or poverty reduction (poverty). In contrast, a causal-comparative analysis emphasizes how much the outcome (poverty) varies depending on the nominal-type predictor factors. For instance, What percentage of poverty does [predictor] predict (relate to)? (correlational); How does poverty vary depending on [predictor]? (Comparative causality)

The definition of poverty has an impact on what should be measured, how it should be quantified, and how to find poverty reduction strategies (Gillet, pers com., 2002). In an effort to close the gap between earlier professional definitions and local views, this has led to the awareness that new ways are required to deal with this more nuanced understanding. In order to comprehend poverty more thoroughly, it must be seen as partially the product of structural elements of the social system. The author outlines four broad theories for explaining poverty: poverty as a consequence of innate personal qualities, poverty as a result of contingent personal characteristics, poverty as a result of societal causes, and poverty as a result of innate social system characteristics.

The author then develops a class exploitation theory of poverty by describing how economic oppression, economic exploitation, and class create a social structure in which poverty is a fundamentally important functioning element. The general issue of poverty must be divided into two smaller issues: poverty produced by oppression other than exploitative relations (the underclass) and poverty produced by exploitative relations (the working poor). One of the recommendations is to keep researching other quantitative aspects that can affect poverty statistics in either a positive or negative way. These findings support social transformation by potentially providing monetary policymakers with more focused approaches to reducing poverty levels.
