16. what does a totem pole in British Columbia have in common with a stone circle in the south of England?​


Answer:

I have seen both and I think the only commonality is that they were built by specific tribes in specific areas.

Explanation:

Totem poles are monuments created by First Nations of the Pacific Northwest to represent and commemorate ancestry, histories, people, or events. Totem poles are typically created out of red cedar, a malleable wood relatively abundant in the Pacific and would be erected to be visible within a community.

Most totem poles display beings, or crest animals, marking a family’s lineage and validating the powerful rights and privileges that the family held. Totem poles would not necessarily tell a story so much as it would serve to document stories and histories familiar to community members or particular family or clan members.

They are specific to the region found in BC and Alaska primarily.

The Stone Circles are many thousands of years old, erected by people in prehistoric times - estimates of 5000 - 6000 years ago. The meaning of the stones, and how they were erected are still a mystery which keep historians fascinated.

What they have in common is not easy to classify as there are a number of theories. Basically the totems represent(ed) a family or a tribe. The stone circles, it is thought, represented something greater.
