11. luke 18:18-27 reflection ​


Answer:

f Jesus had taken an evangelism training course, He would have dealt differently with the rich young ruler. From an evangelist’s point of view, this guy was a piece of cake. His eagerness is evident from the fact that (Mark 10:17 reports) he ran, not walked, up to Jesus. He even knelt down before Jesus, right in front of others, and asked, “What must I do to inherit eternal life?” Jesus didn’t even have to figure out how to turn the conversation to spiritual things! What an opportunity! Shouldn’t be too hard to get a decision!

And the man was a choice prospect. Matthew tells us that he was young. He still had most of his adult life ahead of him. He was a ruler (Luke 18:18). The term is not specific, but it points to someone in a position of authority, either in the religious or civil community. He was in a place of influence in spite of his youthfulness. And, he was extremely rich (Luke 18:23). With just a tithe, he could have bankrolled Jesus’ mission for years to come. What a key person! But Jesus let him walk away unconverted.

Not only that, but the man was from a good background. He didn’t have any serious problems to overcome—no drugs or alcohol, no history of trouble with the law. From his youth, he had tried to keep the Ten Commandments, and he had done a pretty good job of it. He was a fine young man, the kind that any church would lift up as an example. It shouldn’t take much to lead this man to Christ. But Jesus seemed to take the wrong approach!

25. lessons from luke 18:9-14​


Answer:

There are several lessons that can be drawn from the passage in Luke 18:9-14. Here are a few potential interpretations:

Humility is important: In this passage, the tax collector acknowledges his own sinfulness and asks for God's mercy, while the Pharisee boasts about his own righteousness. The tax collector is ultimately justified, while the Pharisee is not. This suggests that humility is an important virtue and that it is important to recognize our own limitations and faults.

God is merciful: The tax collector is justified not because of his own merit, but because he seeks God's mercy and forgiveness. This suggests that God is a merciful and forgiving God, who is willing to extend grace to those who turn to him.

Pride can be a hindrance: The Pharisee's pride in his own righteousness ultimately leads to his own downfall. This suggests that pride can be a hindrance to one's spiritual growth and that it is important to recognize our own limitations and faults.

God sees the heart: In this passage, it is not the outward appearance or actions of the tax collector or the Pharisee that matter, but rather their heart and their relationship with God. This suggests that God sees and knows the heart, and that it is important to be sincere in our faith.

Overall, these lessons highlight the importance of humility, recognizing our own sinfulness, seeking God's mercy, and being sincere in our faith.
