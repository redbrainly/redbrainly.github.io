4. ARGUMENTATIVE ESSAYDefine the following. Give 1 example of each.1. CLAIM2. EVIDENCE 3. COUNTER-ARGUMENT 4. REBUTTAL5. CONCLUSION ​​


Answer:

What Are the Five Parts of an Argumentative Essay?

A Well-Structured Thesis. Write an introductory paragraph that introduces your argument and explains why readers should be interested in your topic. ...

Supporting Body Paragraphs. ...

Your Persuasive Conclusion. ...

Transitional Phrases.

Answer:

1. Claim - An essay's core argument is known as a claim. It is, without a doubt, the most significant aspect of a research paper. The claim determines the paper's complexity, effectiveness, and quality. The rest of the paper will most likely be boring or obvious if your claim is boring or apparent.

Example: Because of its texture, creamy filling, and golden hue, Twinkies taste better than other snack cakes.

2. Evidence - Facts, documentation, or testimony used to support a claim, support an argument, or establish a conclusion are referred to as evidence in an argument.

Example: "Last year, my cousin wrecked her car off a bridge and would have drowned if she had been wearing her seatbelt," a proponent of seat belt use would state as evidence (the answer to the question "Why do you believe that?").

Because there are many more car wrecks that do not result in water, an opponent would argue that this scenario is not a good assessment of the relative safety of not using a seat belt in this situation.

3. Counter-Argument - A counterargument is a statement that acknowledges opposing viewpoints and then reaffirms your own. In argumentative essays, the counterargument is a common academic technique that tells the reader that you can understand and respect both sides of an argument.

Example: If you claim that cats are better pets because they are more self-sufficient than dogs, and I argue that dogs are better pets because they are more social, your viewpoint is a counterargument to mine.

4. Rebuttal - A rebuttal is the part of a dispute where you point out the flaws in the opposing side's argument. In any case, the key to an effective rebuttal is to show that the opposing argument is irrelevant or contains logical fallacies.

5.Conclusion - if you write a paper about zoo animals, each paragraph would probably be about one particular animal. In your conclusion, you should briefly mention each animal again.

5. What is the Definition of 1.Evidence 2.Conclusion 3.thesis statement 4.counter argument 5.rebuttal/refutation


Answer:

1. Evidence - the available body of facts or information indicating whether a belief or proposition is true or valid.

2. a judgment or decision reached by reasoning.

3. The thesis statement is a one or two sentence encapsulation of your paper's main point, main idea, or main message. Your paper's thesis statement will be addressed and defended in the body paragraphs and the conclusion.

4. an argument or set of reasons put forward to oppose an idea or theory developed in another argument.

5. rebuttal - When two people debate, one of them makes an argument, and the other follows with a rebuttal, which, plainly put, is the "no, you're wrong and this is why" argument.

refutation- the action of proving a statement or theory to be wrong or false.

The claim is that physical education is not a necessary part of the high school curriculum and should be removed. The argument is that physical education classes are  a waste of time and resources, as they do not provide significant educational benefits.

The opponents to this argument point out that physical activity is an important part of overall health and development, and that physical education classes provide students with the opportunity to learn important life skills such as teamwork, cooperation, and sportsmanship.
