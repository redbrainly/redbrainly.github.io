The Bayanihan Mindset of Filipinos

News Article

People were worried about upcoming issues stemming from the potential loss of employment and other sources of income as the lockdown this year continued. Due to the restrictions placed on people's mobility, many businesses had to close, which resulted in individuals losing their jobs and income.

But because to one person standing up to inspire the bayanihan spirit in everyone, people have discovered a new way to strengthen themselves rather than falling into desperation. An entrepreneur and University of the Philippines alumna named Ana Patricia "Patreng" Non inspired the bayanihan spirit by setting up a small cart outside of her home in Maginhawa, Quezon City, where people could buy food and other items to get by for the day and share any extras that could be used by another family.

This straightforward act of sharing and giving has inspired farmers and other organizations to donate, and it has developed into the Maginhawa Community Pantry. Since then, people from all over the Philippines have started to establish community pantries in their neighborhoods, turning this modest act of kindness into a movement.

More than 200 hundred pantries had been established by May, and the campaign has inspired others to establish community pantries as far away as our neighbor, Timor Leste. The community pantry movement may have seen obstacles and criticism, but Patreng's efforts to inspire people to support their livelihoods by assisting one another are too admirable to fail.
