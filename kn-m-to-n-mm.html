4. A 10 kn car is to be lifted by a hydraulic jack. if 625 n force is applied in a 25-mm diameter plunger, find the required diameter of the piston in mm.


Hydraulic Pascal's Law Problem

The answer is 126 mm.

A mechanical process known as hydraulic uses the force of liquid pressure to operate. In hydraulics-based systems, confined, pumped liquid moves pistons, often through hydraulic cylinders, to create mechanical movement.

Formula:

F1 / A1 = F2 / A2

Where,

• F1 is the force applied to the plunger

• A1 is the area of the plunger

• F2 is the force exerted by the piston

•[tex]A2 = 625 N * (π * (0.025 m)^2) / 10 kN= 0.0039 m^2[/tex] A2 is the area of the piston

We can rearrange the formula to solve for the area of the piston:

A2 = F1 * A2 / F2

Solution:

Substituting the given values:

[tex]A2 = 625 N * (\pi * (0.025 m)^2) / 10 kN= 0.0039 m^2[/tex]

The area of the piston is 0.0039 square meters. To find the diameter of the piston in millimeters, we can convert the area to diameter using the formula for the area of a circle:

[tex]A=\pi * r^{2}[/tex]

Solving for the radius r:

√[tex](A/\pi )[/tex]

Substituting the values:

r[tex]=[/tex]√[tex](0.0039 m^2 / \pi )[/tex]

[tex]= 0.063 m[/tex]

The radius of the piston is 0.063 meters. To find the diameter, we can multiply the radius by 2, which gives us a diameter of:

[tex]diameter = 2 * 0.063 m\\= 0.126 m[/tex]

Converting to millimeters:

diameter = 0.126 m * 1000 mm/m

= 126 mm

R = [tex]\sqrt{(\Sigma x)^2+(\Sigma y)^2}[/tex]

where

∑x = 0.45P₁ + 0.6P₂ - 20 kN

0.45P₁ + 0.6P₂ - 20 = 0

0.89P₁ - 0.67P₂ = 0          

Now, let us substitute the horizontal and vertical forces to the formula for resultant forces

R = [tex]\sqrt{(\Sigma x)^2+(\Sigma y)^2}[/tex]

R² = ∑x² + ∑y²

40² = (0.45P₁ + 0.6P₂ - 20)² + (0.89P₁ - 0.67P₂)²       equation 1

Let us use another formula for the resultant's angle.

tan θ = ∑y / ∑x

0.58 (0.45P₁ + 0.6P₂ - 20) = 0.89P₁ - 0.67P₂

- 0.63P₁ + 1.02P₂ = 11.6

Substitute to equation 1

19. A steel bar is 10 mm diameter and 2 m long. It is stretched with a force of 20 kN and extends by 0.2 mm. Calculate the stress and strain.


Answer:

a. The stress is 2.55 x 10⁷ Pa

b. The strain is 1 x 10 ⁻⁴

Explanation:

Here, we are to solve for the stress and strain of a steel bar. To do this we will needing the following formula:

a. σ = F/A

where

σ     is the stress, unit is in N/m² or Pascal, Pa

F     is the force, unit is in Newton, N

A     is the cross-sectional area, unit is in m²

b. ε = ΔL/L₀

where

ε     is the strain, unitless

ΔL   is the change in length

L₀    is the original length

For the given information

d = 10 mm = 0.01 m

L₀ = 2 m

F = 20 kN = 20 x 10³ N

ΔL = 0.2 mm = 0.0002 m

Solving the problem

a. First, let us solve for the cross-sectional area of the steel bar.

A = πd²/4

A = π (0.01 m)²/4 = 7.85 x 10⁻⁵ m²

Now, let us solve for stress.

σ = F/A

σ = 20 x 10³ N/ 7.85 x 10⁻⁵ m²

σ = 2.55 x 10⁷ Pa

b. Now, let solve for the strain of the steel bar using the 2nd given formula.

ε = ΔL/L₀

ε = 0.0002 m / 2 m

ε = 0.0001 or

ε = 1 x 10 ⁻⁴

Therefore, the stress and the strain of the steel bar are 2.55 x 10⁷ Pa and   1 x 10 ⁻⁴ respectively.

28. The surface tensions of mercury and water at 60C are 0. 47 N/m and 0. 0662 N/m, respectively. What capillary-height changes will occur in these two fluids when they are in contact with air in a glass tube of radius 0. 30 mm? Use angle of contact = 130 deg. For mercury, and 0 deg. For water; specific weight of mercury is 132. 3 kN/m3 and 9. 650 kN/m3 for water.


To calculate the capillary-height change in these two fluids, we can use the following equation:

Δh = 2γcosθ / ρgr

Where:

Δh is the capillary-height change

γ is the surface tension of the fluid

θ is the angle of contact between the fluid and the glass

ρ is the density of the fluid

g is the acceleration due to gravity

r is the radius of the glass tube

For mercury, we can plug in the values given in the question to get:

Δh = 2 * 0.47 N/m * cos(130 deg) / (132.3 kN/m3 * 9.81 m/s2 * 0.30 mm)

= 4.88 x 10-6 m

For water, we can plug in the values given in the question to get:

Δh = 2 * 0.0662 N/m * cos(0 deg) / (9.650 kN/m3 * 9.81 m/s2 * 0.30 mm)

= 1.29 x 10-6 m

Therefore, the capillary-height change in mercury will be 4.88 x 10-6 m and the capillary-height change in water will be 1.29 x 10-6 m.
