1) (E) In stitches

→ Laughing uncontrollably

Origin:

Even if the exact phrase does not appear before 1930, Shakespeare used a similar one in Twelfth Night (3 : 2) : If you want to make yourself laugh so hard you weep, come with me.  Stitches relate to a sudden pain in the side, often known as a "stitch," which, like a fit of laughter, can cause a person to double over.

2) (C) Tied the knot

→ To get married.

Origin:

This a reference to the handfasting ceremony, an old custom in which the bride and groom's hands were tied together with pieces of cloth, cord, rope, etc. as a sign that their marriage would last.

The word "knot" has been linked to marriage for a very long time. The first known use of the word "knot" was in 1225. It is not clear if the knot refers to a real knot that is tied during a wedding or if it is just a metaphor for two people coming together. In 1717, an English poet named Matthew Prior used this exact phrase in his poem "Alma; or, The Progress of the Mind."

3) (A) A red letter day

→ A day that was both happy and important.

Origin:

In ancient Rome, important days were marked with red ever since the Middle Ages. On many calendars, important dates are still written in red instead of black. Some people think that the church in 325 AD printed Holy Days in red, but there isn't much evidence to support this. In the United Kingdom, dates that were important to the government, as well as the church, were written in red on the calendar. People started calling all of these days "the red letter day."

4) (B) Eavesdropping

→ Eavesdropping is the act of listening in secret or stealthily to someone else's private conversation or communication without their permission.

Origin:

The phrase "to eavesdrop" dates back to the early 1600s and comes from the eaves of a house, which are the parts of the roof that hang over the walls and keep rain from getting into the walls. The water would always drip from the eaves, so the space between the wall and the roof's extended edge came to be called the eavesdrip, and then the eavesdrop. So, at first, it meant a part of the house. Soon, though, it became a part of the house where nosy people could stand under cover and listen in on private conversations inside the house. By the 1600s, this kind of thing was called "eavesdropping."

5) (D) Under the weather

→ Sick or unwell

Origin:

The term "under the weather," which means sick or feeling worse than usual, comes from the days when sailing ships were used. If a sailor was sick, he would be sent below deck, where he would be safe from the weather. (If the sailor was below deck, he or she would be under the weather.)
