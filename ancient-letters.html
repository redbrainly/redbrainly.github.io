11. Chinese Literature What is an ancient house? an ancient wall? an ancient face? an ancient debt?


The houses are referred to in ancient China.

Throughout Chinese history, houses, palaces, temples, monasteries, family businesses, and government offices utilized the siheyuan composition. In ancient times, a siheyuan was occupied by a single, typically large, extended family, symbolizing riches and success.

The ancient Chinese wall

Emperor Qin Shi Huang first envisioned the Great Wall in the third century B.C. to deter attacks by nomadic barbarians. As a result, it is perhaps the most iconic emblem of China and its vast and colorful history. The Great Wall is the largest artificial structure in the world, spanning thousands of kilometres, but it is not its only significance. It played a significant role in defending China from northern invasions for millennia and is now a national emblem and China's most popular tourist attraction.

The ancient Chinese notion of the face

In Chinese culture, 'face', also known as mianzi (mi-an-ze), refers to the degree of "dignity" or "prestige" that an individual possesses. The second is lian, used frequently in informal speech but less in formal writing.

Face reading or physiognomy is an old Chinese practice that uses various facial features to identify a person's health, personality, abilities, talents, areas of blocked energy or emotions, and compatibility with particular others.

The ancient Chinese understanding of debt

There is a procedure called "Repaying the Loan for Life" in the context of telling Chinese texts. This performance dates back to China's Ming (1368–1644) and Qing (1644–1911) eras when such recitations were famous in many regions. In addition to village temples, they perform at religious gatherings hosted by local believers in private homes. The "Repaying the Loan for Life" ceremony in Changshu is called "Returning of the Scripture on the Loan for Life."

This abbreviated form will be used moving forward. However, its performance is contingent on the notion that each contracts a "Loan for Life" at birth. According to this concept, the debt owed to the underworld's treasury must be returned before death. If not, their soul will be condemned to the torments of the afterlife and a dreadful kind of reincarnation in the following lifetime. To repay this symbolic obligation, the descendants must organize a ritual gathering for their elderly parents.

A meteor is a streak of light in the sky caused by a meteoroid crashing through Earth’s atmosphere.

Meteoroids are lumps of rock or iron that orbit the sun. Most meteoroids are small fragments of rock created by asteroid collisions. Comets also create meteoroids as they orbit the sun and shed dust and debris.

When a meteoroid enters the Earth’s upper atmosphere, it heats up due to friction from the air. The heat causes gases around the meteoroid to glow brightly, and a meteor appears. Meteors are often referred to as shooting stars or falling stars because of the bright tail of light they create as they pass through the sky. Most meteors occur in Earth’s mesosphere, about 50-80 kilometers (31-50 miles) above the Earth's surface.

Even the smallest meteors are visible from many kilometers away because of how fast they travel and how brightly they shine. The fastest meteors travel at speeds of 71 kilometers (44 miles) per second.

The faster and larger the meteor, the brighter and longer it may glow. The smallest meteors only glow for about a second while larger and faster meteors can be visible for up to several minutes. Although thousands of meteors fall during the day, meteors are best observed at night, when the streaks of light are visible in the dark sky.

Meteors appear in different colors, depending on the chemical composition of the space rock and the air it is passing through. A meteor with high iron content, for instance, will appear yellow. A meteor with high iron content, for instance, will appear yellow. A meteor with high calcium content may appear as a purple streak of light.

Scientists think up to 50 metric tons of meteors fall on the Earth each day, but most are no bigger than a pebble. Meteors that don’t burn up in the atmosphere strike Earth’s surface. These meteors are called meteorites.

Gallium and Ruthenium

Explanation:

Gallium-Lecoq de Boisbaudran named the element after France ('Gaul' in Latin) and also himself, since Lecoq, which means 'the rooster' translates to 'Gallus' in Latin. A silvery metallic rooster is shown on a background of an antique map of France. Gallium is a soft, silvery-white metal, similar to aluminium.

Discovered by: Paul-Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran

Origin of the name: The name is derived from the Latin name for France, 'Gallia'

Discovery date: 1875

Ruthenium-Like the other metals of the platinum group, ruthenium is inert to most other chemicals. Russian-born scientist of Baltic-German ancestry Karl Ernst Claus discovered the element in 1844 at Kazan State University and named ruthenium in honor of Ruthenia (one of Medieval Latin names for Russia)
