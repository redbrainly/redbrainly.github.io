Arab and Islamic Traditions

Saudi traditions are rooted in Islamic teachings and Arab customs, which Saudis learn about at an early age from their families and in schools.

The highlights of the year are the holy month of Ramadan and the Hajj (pilgrimage) season, and the national holidays that follow them. The holy month of Ramadan, during which Muslims fast from dawn to dusk, culminates with the Eid-Al-Fitr holiday, in which it is customary to buy presents and clothes for children and visit friends and relatives.

The other highlight is the Hajj season, during which millions of Muslim pilgrims from around the world come to Makkah. The Hajj season concludes with the Eid Al-Adha holiday, in which it is traditional for families to slaughter a sheep in memory of Abraham’s willingness to sacrifice his son.

Arab traditions also play an important role in Saudi life. These age-old traditions have evolved over the millennia and are highly regarded. They include generosity and hospitality, which every Saudi family offers to strangers, friends, and family. The simplest expression of hospitality is coffee – its preparation alone is an intricate cultural tradition, and it is often served in small cups along with dates and sweets. Another gesture of hospitality is the burning of incense (oud) to welcome guests.


7. what are the common elements of art that can be seen in Tibet and Saudi Arabia​


What are the common elements of art that can be seen in Tibet and Saudi Arabia

Bronze, clay or wood is the common elements of art that can be seen. Tibetan artists have played an important part in Tibet's cultural life for over a thousand years. Their contributions have permeated nearly every aspect of life on the Tibetan plateau, from designs for painted furniture to lavish murals in religious buildings. The vast majority of surviving artworks produced before the mid-twentieth century are religious in nature, with the main types being thangka, distemper paintings on canvas, Tibetan Buddhist wall paintings, and miniature bronze sculptures or large ones in clay, stucco, or wood. They were commissioned by religious institutions or devout persons for use in the practice of Tibetan Buddhism and were produced in large workshops by monks and lay artists, the majority of whom are anonymous. Tibet's art can be analyzed in terms of the sources that have influenced it over the years, including other Chinese, Nepalese, Indian, and sacred forms. Many bronzes in Tibet with Pala influences are thought to have been made by Indian sculptors or brought from India.  

Contemporary Tibetan art

Refers to modern Tibetan architecture, or Tibet after 1950. It also refers to Tibetan diaspora art that is specifically political, religious, or personal in nature. Thanka paintings are not the only kind of contemporary Tibetan art. Dealing with dynamic sociopolitical realities on a daily basis.  

Saudi Arabian art

Saudi Arabian art should be considered in light of the country's status as the birthplace of Islam, and it should include both the arts of Bedouin nomads and those of sedentary cultures from areas such as the Hejaz, Tihamah, Asir, and Najd.  Bedouins carved tribal symbols known as "wusum" during ancient times, and they can be found as rock art in Arabia's hills and deserts. Saudi Arabia was subjected to a rock art and epigraphic survey from 1985 to 1990. About 1000 rock art sites were documented during this time period. There were large human and animal faces in the early stages of rock painting, and smaller human and animal faces in the later stages.  

Portable art forms

In Saudi Arabia, there are several portable art types. Metalware, jewelry, pottery, and leatherwork are only a few examples. Also now, many Bedouin women knit, producing brightly colored striped rugs, camel trapping, and tents. The tents are black and made of goat fur combined with sheep wool and camel hair.

12. what principles and elements of artd is dominantly used in the art of saudi arabia​


Answer

Elements of Art that are dominant in Saudi Arabian Art are Shapes, Lines, and Space. Its prominent Principles of Design are Pattern and Balance.

Explanation

Muslim Art or Islam Art uses lines to create geometrical and mechanical patterns for their art and architectural designs. It only uses organic lines to depict floral designs such as leaves, vines, branches, flowers, fruits, and so on. However, Muslim Art doesn't use human and fauna designs such as animals, folklore characters, or beasts, and so on because of Islamic teachings. Muslim/Islam Art can still use letters, Ayah (the equal of "verse" in Quran) and names (of a prominent figures in Islam) as a part of the design since it is not depicting an Image.

Muslim art is heavily influenced by the teachings of Islamic Faith. Like other Abrahamic religions, Islam also forbids the worship of any Images or Idols of man-made gods. It is because any living thing on the earth is just a creation of God and is therefore under his power. This is considered as blasphemy and is punishable by death under the Sharia/Islamic Law.

In summary, Muslim/Islamic Art doesn't contain living organisms as art subjects because of religious beliefs.
