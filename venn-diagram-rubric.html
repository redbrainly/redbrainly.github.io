1. what is the meaning of venn diagram RUBRIC​


A Venn diagram is an illustration that uses circles to show the relationships among things or finite groups of things. Circles that overlap have a commonality while circles that do not overlap do not share those traits. Venn diagrams help to visually represent the similarities and differences between two concepts.

Two-Set Diagrams. This type of a Venn diagram uses two circles or ovals to show overlapping properties.

Three-Set Diagrams. You can always call these three circle diagrams as well.

Four-Set Diagrams. A four-set Venn diagram is one that's packed with four, overlapping sets.

PLANT CELL:

Plant cells have a cell wall and chloroplasts. Plant cells usually have one or more large vacuole(s),

ANIMAL CELL:

Animal cells don't have a cell wall and chloroplasts. Animal cells have smaller vacuoles, if any are present.

SIMILARITIES:

Plant and animal cells are very similar because they are both eukaryotic cells. 23. PERFORMANCE TASK 4 VENN DIAGRAM ABOUT CAUSES OF PLATE MOVEMENTS Complete the Venn Diagram by describing the different theories about plate movements (similarities and differences). Use the scoring rubrics as a guide in doing the task. Mantle Convection Current Slab Pull Ridge Push​


PLATE MOVEMENTSMantle ConvectionMantle convection is the term used to explain how the mantle moves when heat is transferred from the blazing core to the brittle lithosphere. The mantle's temperature gradually drops over extended periods of time as a result of below- and above-surface heating and cooling. Mantle Convection vs Current Slab PullThe parts of the mantle that are closest to the core expand and lose density. Gravity causes denser areas farther from the centre to sink. Convection currents are created in the mantle as a result of these coupled causes. Denser locations sink due to slab pull, forcing hotter and less dense regions upward in a circular motion.Slab PullAlong convergent boundaries and subduction zones, denser oceanic plates descend beneath less dense continental plates, creating a force known as slab pull. The rest of the oceanic plate is pulled along with the section that is suffering slab pull by the gravitational force of the sinking oceanic plate.Slab Pull vs. Ridge PushThe two main forces that are assumed to be moving oceanic plates are "Ridge Push" and "Slab Pull." Due of the ridges' steep terrain, ridge push is a result of potential energy gradients. The subduction zone plate's low buoyancy is what causes slab pull.Ridge PushA plate's movement into a subduction zone as a result of gravitational pull away from an ocean ridge's crest. It complements Slab Pull but is significantly less important.
